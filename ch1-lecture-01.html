Lecture 1: Introduction to Matrices

1. Definition

A matrix is a rectangular arrangement of numbers, symbols, or expressions in rows and columns. It is usually denoted by a capital letter, e.g., \( A \).

2. Notation and Order

If a matrix has \( m \) rows and \( n \) columns, it is called an \( m \times n \) (m by n) matrix. The elements of the matrix are written as \( a_{ij} \), where \( i \) is the row number and \( j \) is the column number.

Example: A \( 2 \times 3 \) matrix:
a11a12a13
a21a22a23

3. Basic Operations

Matrices can be added, subtracted, or multiplied by a scalar.

Matrix Addition:
If \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \), then \( A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 1+5 & 2+6 \\ 3+7 & 4+8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{bmatrix} \).
Scalar Multiplication:
If \( k = 3 \) and \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \), then \( kA = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 9 & 12 \end{bmatrix} \).

4. Applications in Engineering

Matrices are widely used in engineering:
  • Civil Engineering: To solve systems of equations in structural analysis.
  • Electrical Engineering: To analyze circuits using node/mesh equations.
  • Computer Graphics: For transformations like rotation, scaling, and translation.
