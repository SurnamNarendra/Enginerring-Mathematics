Lecture 8 — Cayley–Hamilton Theorem & Diagonalization

Definitions, proofs sketch, and several detailed examples.

Cayley–Hamilton Theorem

Statement

Given a square matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), let \(p_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A)\) be its characteristic polynomial. The theorem states that \(p_A(A) = 0\) (the zero matrix).

Remarks

  • Since \(p_A(\lambda)\) is degree \(n\), the theorem gives a polynomial identity of degree \(n\) in \(A\).
  • It is useful to reduce high powers of \(A\) to lower powers in computations of \(A^k\) or \(A^{-1}\) when \(A\) is invertible.

Worked Example — 2×2 matrix

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix}\). Characteristic polynomial: \(p_A(\lambda) = (\lambda-2)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3\).

Cayley–Hamilton says: \(A^2 - 4A + 3I = 0\).

Compute \(A^2 = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 4 \\ 4 & 5\end{pmatrix}\). Substitution gives \(A^2 - 4A + 3I = 0\), verified.

Worked Example — 3×3 upper-triangular

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}\). Being upper triangular, eigenvalues are \(1,2,3\).

So \(p_A(\lambda) = (\lambda-1)(\lambda-2)(\lambda-3) = \lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 + 11\lambda - 6\).

Cayley–Hamilton: \(A^3 - 6A^2 + 11A - 6I = 0\). This can be checked by computing \(A^2\) and \(A^3\) explicitly (upper-triangular products are straightforward).

Worked Example — 3×3 (non-triangular) with inverse formula

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1\end{pmatrix}\). Suppose its characteristic polynomial is \(p_A(\lambda) = \lambda^3 - 4\lambda^2 + 5\lambda - 2\).

By Cayley–Hamilton we have \(A^3 - 4A^2 + 5A - 2I = 0\). Rearranging gives \(2I = A^3 - 4A^2 + 5A\).

If \(A\) is invertible (here \(\det A = 2 \neq 0\)), multiply both sides by \(A^{-1}\) to obtain \[2A^{-1} = A^2 - 4A + 5I,\] hence \[A^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{2}(A^2 - 4A + 5I).\] This provides an explicit polynomial expression for \(A^{-1}\) using Cayley–Hamilton without performing row-reduction.

Tip: using CH to reduce powers

If \(p_A(\lambda)=\lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+\cdots+c_0\), then \(A^n = -c_{n-1}A^{n-1}-\cdots - c_0 I\), so any higher power can be reduced to a linear combination of \(I,A,\dots,A^{n-1}\).

Diagonalization

Definition

\(A\) is diagonalizable if there exists an invertible \(P\) and diagonal \(D\) such that \(A = P D P^{-1}\).

The diagonal entries of \(D\) are eigenvalues of \(A\); columns of \(P\) are eigenvectors.

Worked Example — Diagonalization (2×2)

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3\end{pmatrix}\). Characteristic polynomial: \(\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda-2)(\lambda-5)\).

Eigenvalues \(2,5\) with eigenvectors \((-1,2)^T\) and \((1,1)^T\). So \(P = \begin{pmatrix}-1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1\end{pmatrix}\) and \(D = \operatorname{diag}(2,5)\).

Non-diagonalizable Example

\(A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}\) has characteristic polynomial \((\lambda-3)^2\) with only one independent eigenvector. Hence not diagonalizable.

Summary: Cayley–Hamilton helps reduce powers and find inverses via polynomial identities. Diagonalization simplifies matrix computations when enough eigenvectors exist.
